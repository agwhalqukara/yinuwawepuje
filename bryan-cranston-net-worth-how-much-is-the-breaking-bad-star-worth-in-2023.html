    Bryan Cranston’s Early Career and Struggles

    Bryan Cranston was born on March 7, 1956, in Hollywood, California, to parents who were both struggling actors.

    He had a difficult childhood, as his father abandoned the family when he was 11 years old, and he had to live with his grandparents.

    He graduated from Los Angeles Valley College with a degree in police science, but he decided to pursue acting after taking some elective classes and going on a cross-country motorcycle trip with his brother.

    Cranston started his acting career in local and regional theaters and then moved on to commercials, films, and TV shows.

    He had small roles in shows like CHiPs, Airwolf, and Hill Street Blues.

    He also did voice-over work for Japanese anime and the first season of Mighty Morphin Power Rangers.

    His first big break came when he was cast as a recurring character on the hit sitcom Seinfeld, playing Jerry’s dentist Dr. Tim Whatley.

    Bryan Cranston’s Rise to Fame with Malcolm in the Middle and Breaking Bad

    In 2000, Cranston landed a starring role as Hal, the goofy and loving father of four boys, in the Fox sitcom Malcolm in the Middle.

    The show was a huge success, running for seven seasons and earning Cranston three Emmy nominations for Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Comedy Series.

    Cranston also directed several episodes of the show and wrote one.

    In 2008, Cranston was cast as Walter White in Breaking Bad, a dark and gritty drama about a high school chemistry teacher who starts making methamphetamine to secure his family’s future after being diagnosed with terminal lung cancer.

    The show was created by Vince Gilligan, who had worked with Cranston on an episode of The X-Files and was impressed by his range and versatility.

    Breaking Bad became one of the most critically acclaimed and popular shows of all time, winning 16 Emmy Awards and two Golden Globe Awards.

    Cranston’s performance as Walter White was widely praised as one of the best in TV history, earning him four Emmy Awards for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Drama Series, two Golden Globe Awards for Best Actor in a Television Series – Drama, and three Screen Actors Guild Awards for Outstanding Performance by a Male Actor in a Drama Series.

    Bryan Cranston’s Other Projects and Achievements

    Besides Malcolm in the Middle and Breaking Bad, Cranston has also appeared in many other TV shows, such as Sneaky Pete (which he co-created and produced), Curb Your Enthusiasm (for which he received an Emmy nomination), Your Honor (for which he received a Golden Globe nomination), and Better Call Saul (a spin-off of Breaking Bad).

    He has also lent his voice to animated shows like Family Guy, Robot Chicken, Archer, and SuperMansion.

    Cranston has also starred in many films, such as Saving Private Ryan (1998), Little Miss Sunshine (2006), Drive (2011), Contagion (2011), Argo (2012), Godzilla (2014), Trumbo (2015), The Infiltrator (2016), The Upside (2017), and Asteroid City (2023).

    He received an Academy Award nomination for Best Actor for his role as Dalton Trumbo, a blacklisted Hollywood screenwriter who wrote under pseudonyms during the McCarthy era.

    Cranston has also ventured into theater, winning two Tony Awards for Best Actor in a Play for his portrayals of President Lyndon B. Johnson in All the Way (2014) and Howard Beale, a news anchor who goes mad on air, in Network (2019).

    He also won an Olivier Award for Best Actor for his role in Network on the West End.

    Bryan Cranston’s Personal Life and Philanthropy

    Bryan Cranston has been married twice.

    His first marriage was to writer Mickey Middleton from 1977 to 1982.

    His second marriage is to actor Robin Dearden, whom he met on the set of Airwolf in 1984.

    They have a daughter named Taylor Dearden Cranston, who is also an actor and appeared as one of Walter White’s students in Breaking Bad.

    Cranston is an avid baseball fan and supports the Los Angeles Dodgers.

    He is also involved in various charitable causes, such as the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (for which he produced an instructional DVD called KidSmartz), Autism Speaks, Broadway Cares/Equity Fights AIDS, and the Kind Campaign (an anti-bullying nonprofit that he and his Breaking Bad co-star Aaron Paul raised $1 million for in 2013).
